How I Validate a Digital Product Before Building It

The most expensive mistake in digital products isn't a bad design. It's spending two weeks building something nobody was ever going to buy. I've learned to spend a few hours up front making sure demand exists — before I write a single line of the actual product. Here's the exact check I run.

It's not fancy. It's four questions, answered with free tools, in an afternoon.

Question 1: Does anyone already pay for something like this?

Counter-intuitively, you want to find competitors. A market with paying competitors is a market with proven demand. A market with none usually means one of two things: you're brilliantly early, or — far more often — nobody wants it.

So the first move is to search the marketplaces (Gumroad, Etsy, and a plain search engine) for products adjacent to your idea and look for signs of money changing hands: reviews, ratings, "X sold" counts. When I looked into a content-repurposing tool, the global category was obviously alive — established paid tools, prompt packs with real reviews. That was the green light for demand.

Question 2: Where's the gap they're not filling?

Proven demand isn't enough on its own — if you build the exact same thing as ten established players, you lose. So the next question is what those competitors don't do.

I list what exists and look for a specific combination nobody offers. For the repurposing tool, the existing options were mostly monthly-subscription software or generic prompt packs. The gap I found was a specific packaging: an installable skill (not a subscription), that does the whole job in one pass, set up for non-technical people. Same proven demand, different shape — that's the opening.

Question 3: What will people actually pay, and does the math work?

Before building, I pin down the realistic price by looking at what comparable products charge — not what I hope to charge. If similar products sell for €9–€25, that's my ceiling, no matter how much work I plan to put in. Then I ask the uncomfortable question: at that price, is this worth building? If a realistic price times a realistic number of sales doesn't add up to something worth an afternoon, I kill the idea right there. Better to kill it on paper than after two weeks of work.

Question 4: The check that saved me the most

Here's the one most people skip, and it's the most valuable: is the market I'm targeting actually spending money, or just present?

When I researched my repurposing tool, I nearly built it Spanish-first, because that felt like a less crowded niche. But when I actually inspected the Spanish-language paid market for this kind of AI/prompt product, the listings had almost no reviews or sales — lots of interest, almost no buying. The English market, meanwhile, had clear evidence of people paying. That single check flipped my whole plan: build English-first, include Spanish, and don't stake the launch on an audience that browses but doesn't buy.

That's the difference between a market that exists and a market that pays. They are not the same thing, and confusing them is how people build beautiful products into silence.

The rule I follow now

I don't let myself build until all four questions pass: real demand, a real gap, price math that works, and a market that actually spends. It feels slow when you're excited about an idea. It's the opposite — it's what stops you burning weeks on ideas that were never going to sell, so the weeks you do spend go into things that can.

Validation isn't the boring part before the real work. For a solo maker with limited time, it is the highest-leverage work you'll do. An afternoon of checking beats a fortnight of building the wrong thing.
