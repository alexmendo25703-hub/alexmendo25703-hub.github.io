Gumroad vs Etsy for Selling Templates: What I'd Choose Starting From Zero

If you're a faceless creator about to sell your first Notion template, planner, or digital download, you hit this fork immediately: Gumroad or Etsy? The answer isn't "the popular one" — it depends on one thing most guides skip: where your traffic comes from.

Here's how I'd decide, and why.

The one question that actually settles it

Etsy and Gumroad aren't really competitors. They're two different business models wearing similar clothes:

  • Etsy is a marketplace. It has built-in traffic — millions of buyers already searching. You're renting a stall in a crowded market. People find you.
  • Gumroad is a storefront. It has almost no built-in discovery. You bring your own traffic.

So the real question is: do you have a way to send people to your product, or do you need the platform to send them to you?

If you have zero audience and zero traffic plan, Etsy's marketplace can surface you. If you're driving your own traffic (Pinterest, SEO, a blog, an email list), Gumroad lets you keep more and own the relationship.

That's the whole decision in one line.

Fees: closer than people claim

The "Etsy takes so much" reputation is a bit dated — both take a real cut once you add everything up.

  • Etsy: a listing fee per item, plus a transaction fee, plus payment processing, and often mandatory Offsite Ads fees on sales they source.
  • Gumroad: a flat percentage plus a small per-transaction fee, with no monthly fee and no listing fee. You pay when you sell, nothing to list.

Both platforms adjust these rates over time, so check the current numbers before you rely on them. Verdict: roughly a wash for a small catalog. Don't pick on fees — the traffic question matters far more.

Discovery: Etsy's real advantage

This is where Etsy earns its cut. Someone types "budget Notion template" into Etsy search and finds you — no ads, no audience, no Pinterest strategy. For a complete beginner with no traffic, that's genuinely valuable.

The catch: you're one of thousands, ranked by Etsy's algorithm, competing on price, with buyers who'll never be yours. Etsy owns the customer, and it can change its rules, fees, or your ranking overnight.

Ownership: Gumroad's real advantage

On Gumroad you get the buyer's email, you can build a list, you can upsell, and nobody sits between you and your customer. You own the asset.

The catch: nobody's coming unless you bring them. A Gumroad link with no traffic is a dead page. I learned this the hard way — a product sitting live with no promotion makes exactly zero sales, no matter how good it is.

So here's what I'd actually do

Since I'm building traffic through Pinterest and a blog, I'd start on Gumroad — and it's not close, for my situation:

  • My traffic doesn't need Etsy's marketplace; I'm creating my own with pins and articles.
  • I keep the customer relationship and build an email list — the actual long-term asset.

But I'd tell a different person the opposite. If you have no traffic plan and no interest in building one, Etsy's built-in search is the faster path to a first sale. Be honest about which one you are.

Bottom line

Pick based on traffic, not fees. No audience and no plan to build one: Etsy. Driving your own traffic and want to own your customers: Gumroad. I'm in the second camp, so I build on Gumroad first.

Whichever you choose, the platform is the easy part. The hard part — the part that actually determines whether you sell anything — is getting the right people to the page. No platform does that for you.
